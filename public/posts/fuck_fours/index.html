What if we got rid of the number 4?

Introduction

Here is a little thought I had the other day:

If we got rid of the number 4, what would happen?

Now, I don’t mean that we switch to base 9 and use the symbols 0, 1, 2, 3, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9 for our numbers (though that would be a fun way to confuse people). My disdain for the number 4 runs deeper than this. I want to know what happens when we continue to do math as normal, but we remove all 4’s from existence.

The clearest way of demonstrating what I mean is to look at doubling a number nnn. Normally, doubling nnn gives us 2n2n2n. We can write this as n↦2nn\mapsto 2nn↦2n if you want to use some more fun math notation.

Definition

What I am proposing, for lack of a better way of writing it, is that doubling nnn instead gives us n↦2n∩{0,1,2,3,5,6,7,8,9}n\mapsto 2n\cap\{0,1,2,3,5,6,7,8,9\}n↦2n∩{0,1,2,3,5,6,7,8,9}. In English, you double nnn and then cut out all of the resultant fours. For the sake of future shorthand, I will call this doubling function ddd.

Here are a few examples to help understand what it means to truly experience freedom from fours fury:

  • d(1)=2d(1)=2d(1)=2: Double 1 to get 2. Nothing new here.
  • d(2)=0d(2)=0d(2)=0: Double 2 to get 4, but we remove the 4 and are left with 0.
  • d(32)=6d(32)=6d(32)=6: Double 32 to get 64, remove the 4 to get 6.
  • d(0)=0d(0)=0d(0)=0. That one is pretty self explanatory.

Notice, by simplying culling fours from our figures, we no longer have a monotonically increasing function! In fact, repeated applications of ddd don’t necessarily increase the number.

Hypothesis

After playing around with this for a while, I have come up with the following conjecture.

Conjecture 1: For all numbers nnn, repeated applications of ddd will eventually cause it collapse to 0. In otherwords, for each n∈Nn\in \mathbf{N}n∈N, there exists some k∈Nk\in\mathbf{N}k∈N such that dk(n)=0d^k(n)=0dk(n)=0.

This seems to be the case to me because it takes just over 3 doublings to gain a digit (log⁡2(10)≈3.322\log_2(10)\approx 3.322log2​(10)≈3.322) but only one unlucky occurrence of a 4 to lose a digit. The only counterexample to this conjecture, aside from some nnn exploding to ∞\infty∞, is if there were some loop where repeated applications of ddd looped back around on itself. Essentially, that dk(n)=nd^k(n)=ndk(n)=n for some k>1k>1k>1. The odds of this seem to me to be almost just as low as some nnn managing to explode.

An exhaustive search

I checked all numbers up to 100,000,000 with some fairly unoptimized Python code and found that every single one collapsed to 0 eventually. The longest chain in this search that I found was for 99,999,995 with a chain length of 28. Another hypothesis

The first few lengths here seem to line up with the longest chains from the previous table. That leads me to my next conjecture.

Conjecture 2: The longest chain produced by a starting number under 10k10^k10k is exactly the length of the chain produced by 10k−110^k-110k−1. In other words, c(n)≤c(10k−1)c(n)\leq c(10^k-1)c(n)≤c(10k−1) for all n<10kn<10^kn<10k.

This makes intuitive sense to me after playing around with a few of these, as 9 takes a while to start producing 4’s.
